# Should you rent out a house or sell it? — explained from scratch

*No background needed. We'll build up every idea, and do the math together.*

---

## Part 0: The setup (the actual situation)

Imagine you own a house worth about **$1.5 million**. You don't live in it anymore. You
have two choices:

- **SELL it now**, take the cash, and put that money into the stock market.
- **HOLD it**: rent it out to tenants (they pay you every month), and sell it later.

Which makes you richer in the long run? That's the whole question. To answer it we need a
few ideas first. Don't worry if you've never thought about any of this — we start at zero.

---

## Part 1: Five ideas you need first

### Idea 1: Money grows if you invest it

If you put money in the stock market, it grows over time — historically about **7% a
year** on average. "7% a year" means each year your money becomes 1.07× as big.

The magic part is **compounding**: you earn growth *on your previous growth*. $100 doesn't
become $170 after 10 years — it becomes about **$197**, because each year's 7% is taken on
a bigger and bigger pile.

> **Math check:** $100 growing 7%/year for 10 years = $100 × (1.07)¹⁰.
> 1.07¹⁰ ≈ 1.97, so ≈ **$197**. For 30 years: 1.07³⁰ ≈ 7.6, so $100 → **$760**.
> Time is doing a *lot* of the work. Remember that — it comes back later.

So whichever choice eventually turns into cash, that cash then grows at ~7%/year. This is
the "fair comparison" trick: once *both* choices are just money sitting in the market, they
grow the same way. So the real question is only **how much cash does each choice hand you,
and when?**

### Idea 2: A house can grow in value too ("appreciation")

Houses tend to get more valuable over time. If a house goes up about **4.5% a year**, we
say it *appreciates* 4.5%/year. Same compounding idea as the stock market, just a different
(usually smaller) rate.

Right away you might think: "Stocks grow 7%, houses grow 4.5%, so just sell and buy stocks,
right?" **Hold that thought** — there's a twist (Idea 4) that flips it.

### Idea 3: A mortgage = you only paid for *part* of the house

Almost nobody buys a house with their own cash. You borrow most of it from a bank. That
loan is a **mortgage**. So out of a $1.5M house, maybe you only actually own about **$390k
of it** — your slice is called your **equity**. The bank "owns" the other ~$1.1M (the loan)
until you pay it back.

You pay the bank back slowly every month, plus interest. The interest rate on this
particular loan is very low — **2.25%/year**. (Loans below the stock market's 7% are
basically cheap money. Keep that in mind.)

### Idea 4: Leverage — the twist that makes houses powerful

Here's the key trick, and it's worth slowing down for.

The house appreciates on its **whole value** ($1.5M), but **your slice is only $390k.** So
when the house goes up 4.5%, you personally gain:

> 4.5% of $1.5M = **$67,500**.

But that $67,500 gain is on *your* $390k stake. As a percentage of *your* money:

> $67,500 ÷ $390,000 ≈ **17%**.

**Whoa.** The house only went up 4.5%, but *you* went up ~17%, because you're riding the
appreciation of the *whole* house while having only put down a sliver. That's called
**leverage** — using borrowed money to multiply your gains. (It multiplies losses too, but
set that aside for now.)

So now compare again:
- Sell → stocks → **7%/year**.
- Hold → your leveraged equity → **~17%/year** (early on!).

Suddenly holding looks *much* better. So why would anyone ever sell? Because — and this is
the heart of the whole thing — **that 17% doesn't last.**

### Idea 5: Taxes (the government takes a cut when you sell)

When you sell something for a profit, the government taxes the profit. Two pieces matter:

- **Capital-gains tax:** sell the house (or stocks) for more than you paid, and you owe tax
  on the gain — here, about **37%** of the profit.
- **The $250k home exclusion (a special rule):** the government lets you keep the first
  **$250,000** of profit on a home *tax-free* — BUT only if you sold it recently enough
  after living in it. The rule: you must have lived in it 2 of the last 5 years. Practically,
  that means **you keep this tax break only if you sell within about 3 years.** Sell in year
  4 or later, and *poof* — the break is gone and you owe full tax. (This rule is called
  "Section 121," but the name doesn't matter — just remember: **a big tax break that expires
  around year 3.**)

That's all the background. Now we can actually answer the question.

---

## Part 2: Why holding gets worse over time (the core insight)

Remember that ~17% leveraged return? Watch what happens to it.

Every year, two things shrink it:

1. **Your equity slice grows.** As the house appreciates and you pay down the loan, your
   $390k stake becomes $450k, then $550k, then more. The same house-level gain, divided by a
   *bigger* stake, is a *smaller* percentage. The leverage advantage fades.
2. **The loan gets paid off.** Once you've mostly paid the bank back, you're no longer
   getting that cheap-borrowed-money boost. You just own a house growing at its plain 4.5%.

So your personal growth rate starts around **17%**, and **slides down year after year**,
heading toward the house's plain **4.5%**.

Now put that next to the stock market's flat **7%**:

| Year you'd sell | Your house-equity is growing about… | vs. stocks (7%) |
|---|---|---|
| 1–3 (early) | ~16–17% | **House wins** |
| ~5 | ~7% (about even) | tie |
| ~10 | ~5.7% | **Stocks win** |
| ~20+ | ~5% and falling | **Stocks win** |

**This is the whole answer in one picture.** Early on, leverage makes the house crush
stocks. But that edge *decays*. At some point your house-growth drops below 7%, and from
then on, every extra year you hold, you'd have been richer just selling and buying stocks.
**And once it crosses below 7%, it never comes back up.**

So the rule is simple:

> **Keep holding while your house-equity grows faster than the stock market. Sell the moment
> it drops below.**

---

## Part 3: Putting the tax break and the leverage together

Here's where it gets neat. Two totally separate clocks both happen to point at **year 3**:

- **The leverage clock:** at 4.5% appreciation, your equity's growth rate sinks below the
  stock market's 7% at around year 3–5. After that, holding loses.
- **The tax clock:** the $250,000 tax-free break expires at about year 3. Sell by year 3,
  keep it. Sell later, lose it (a ~$75,000 hit).

They line up. The year your leverage edge runs out is *also* the last year you keep the big
tax break. So the sweet spot is: **rent it out for ~2–3 years, then sell** — you grab the
last of the leverage boost *and* you sell while the tax break is still alive.

**Important:** this "2–3 years" answer is a *coincidence of these particular numbers*, not a
universal law. Change the inputs and the answer moves (next part).

---

## Part 4: It's not always "hold 2–3 years" — there are three cases

It all depends on **how fast you think the house will appreciate.** Three situations:

1. **House barely appreciates (under ~1.75%/year):** even the early leverage boost can't beat
   stocks' 7%. → **Just sell now.**
2. **House appreciates moderately (~1.75% to ~5.18%/year):** leverage beats stocks for a few
   years, then fades. → **Hold ~2–3 years, then sell.** ← *this house, at 4.5%, is here.*
3. **House appreciates fast (over ~5.18%/year):** the house out-grows stocks the *whole* time
   and never drops below. → **Hold onto it for the long haul.**

So before anything else, you have to guess **which bucket your house is in.** That guess
(the appreciation rate) is the single most important number in the whole decision.

> *Where do 1.75% and 5.18% come from?* They're just the appreciation rates where your
> leveraged house-growth crosses the 7% stock line — the boundaries between the three buckets.
> If stocks grew faster than 7%, both boundaries would shift up (you'd lean toward selling
> more).

---

## Part 5: The catch — the math says "hold 2–3 years," but real life adds risk

The math above gives an *average-case* answer. Real life is messier, and these are the costs
the clean number hides. You have to weigh them yourself.

### Catch 1: Bad luck doesn't have time to average out (the biggest one)

Being a landlord has random bad events: a tenant stops paying and you have to evict them, the
house needs a surprise $40,000 repair, or it sits empty for months with no rent. On average
these cost a predictable amount. **But "on average" only works over a long time.**

Think of it like flipping a coin. Flip it 1,000 times and you'll get close to 50% heads. Flip
it **3 times** and you might get all tails. Bad luck *averages out over many years, but not
over just 3.*

So if you only hold for 3 years, you're rolling the dice a small number of times. One bad
year can be brutal:

- A normal year already costs you about **$41,000** out of your own pocket (the rent doesn't
  fully cover the mortgage + expenses).
- A *bad* year — empty house + eviction + big repair all at once — costs about **$86,000.**

If that one bad year lands during your short 3-year hold, it eats a huge chunk of your
profit. The shorter you plan to hold, the more this risk matters. **The math doesn't charge
you for this bad-luck variance — you have to judge whether you could absorb an $86k surprise.**

### Catch 2: It's actual work

Being a landlord for 3 years isn't passive. You deal with tenants, repairs, paperwork, legal
rules about renting and evicting, and the headache of selling a house that has people living
in it. The math counts the *cash* costs but not your *time and stress*. That's a real cost you
subtract yourself.

### Catch 3: The whole edge depends on that cheap loan

Remember the magic 17% came from leverage — borrowing at a cheap **2.25%** while the house
grows faster. If the loan rate were higher, or you'd borrowed less, that early boost shrinks
and the answer tilts toward selling sooner. The strategy is **fragile**: it works *because*
the borrowed money is cheap.

### Catch 4: You might have to dip into savings to be a landlord

Landlords keep an emergency fund (here, about **$90,000**) for those bad years. If you don't
have that cash lying around, you'd have to sell some stocks to get it — and selling stocks
triggers a little tax too. It's a small extra cost, but it only applies to the "hold"
choice, nudging things slightly toward selling.

---

## Part 6: So how do you actually decide?

1. **Make your best guess for how fast the house will appreciate.** This is the big one.
2. **Find your bucket:**
   - Under ~1.75%/year → **sell now.**
   - ~1.75% to ~5.18%/year → **hold ~2–3 years, then sell.**
   - Over ~5.18%/year → **hold long-term.**
3. **If you land in the middle bucket, sanity-check it against the catches:**
   - Could I survive an ~$86,000 bad year over a short hold without it wrecking my profit?
   - Is the extra money worth 2–3 years of being a landlord?
   - Am I sure that cheap loan is going to stick around?
4. **Test how sure you are.** Nudge your appreciation guess up a bit and down a bit. If the
   answer (the bucket) stays the same across guesses you find believable → decide
   confidently. If a small change in your guess flips the bucket → the honest truth is "this
   depends on the housing market more than on anything I can control," and *that's* the real
   takeaway.

---

## The whole thing in one breath

> Your house gives you a big head start over stocks **early**, because borrowed money lets
> your small slice ride the whole house's growth (~17% vs. stocks' 7%). But that head start
> **fades every year** as you own more of the house outright. Sell while you're still ahead.
> For this house, that's around **year 3** — which also happens to be the deadline for a big
> tax break — *as long as* you believe the house will appreciate somewhere in the 2–5%/year
> range, and you're okay with the bad-luck risk and the work of renting it out for those few
> years.
