# Rental Property Tax Deductions: Depreciation, Repairs, and Interest

*A reference summary for a residential rental property owner located in San Francisco, California. Covers federal rules, California-specific treatment, San Francisco-specific costs/taxes, and a property-specific section for a single-family-home primary-residence conversion (Section 9). Every substantive claim is grounded in a cited source (IRS, California FTB, San Francisco ordinances/agencies, or tax-professional commentary), with URLs listed in the Sources section. This is general information, not personalized tax advice.*

## 1. Depreciation (federal)

Depreciation lets you recover the cost of the rental building over a fixed period by deducting a portion each year. You do not shorten or "deduct a year off" the period — the period is fixed by law.

**Core rules:**

- **Recovery period — 27.5 years, straight-line.** Residential rental property is depreciated over 27.5 years using the straight-line method under MACRS, which is required for all rental property placed in service after 1986. [IRS Pub 527; Pub 925/Form 8582 framework] This works out to roughly 3.636% of the building's depreciable basis per year. [Stessa; landlordstudio]
- **Land is not depreciable.** Only the building and qualifying improvements depreciate; you must separate land value out of the purchase price first (commonly via the property tax assessment's land/building split or an appraisal). [Amerisave; firstexchange]
- **Eligibility — four requirements.** You must own the property, use it to produce income, and the property must have a determinable useful life of more than one year. [TurboTax; landlordstudio]
- **When it starts — "placed in service."** Depreciation begins when the property is ready and available to rent, not the closing/purchase date and not when a tenant actually moves in. Example: buy Feb 1, available for rent March 15 → clock starts March 15. [IRS Pub 527; Amerisave]
- **When it ends.** When you have fully recovered your cost basis (after ~27.5 years) or you remove the property from service. [TurboTax; turbotenant]
- **Mid-month convention.** Residential rental property uses a mid-month convention: property placed in service (or disposed of) during any month is treated as placed in service at the midpoint of that month, so the first and last years are prorated. [IRS Pub 527]
- **Where it's reported.** Schedule E (Form 1040), line 18; Form 4562 is used to figure/report depreciation, especially the first year placed in service or any year an improvement is added. [IRS Topic 414; NSKT Global]

**Calculation example:** Buy a single-family rental for $350,000; assessment allocates 25% to land ($87,500) and 75% to building ($262,500). Depreciable basis = $262,500. Annual depreciation = $262,500 ÷ 27.5 = **$9,545/year**, deducted on Schedule E line 18. [NSKT Global]

**Cost basis includes** the purchase price plus certain acquisition costs — legal fees, title insurance, recording fees, transfer taxes, and any seller debts you agree to assume. It does **not** include loan-related costs such as origination fees, points, or mortgage interest (those are handled separately). [IRS Pub 527; landlordstudio]

## 2. The Income Limitation — Passive Activity Loss Rules (the key catch)

Rental real estate is treated as a **passive activity**, so rental losses (including those created by depreciation) generally can only offset passive income, **not** W-2 wages or active business income. This rule has applied since 1986. [IRS Pub 925; turbotenant]

**Important nuance — depreciation itself has no income limit.** You calculate and claim depreciation every year regardless of income. The income rules only affect whether a *resulting loss* can offset your *other (non-passive)* income this year; disallowed losses are suspended, not lost. [Stessa]

### The $25,000 special allowance

If you **actively participate** in managing the rental, you may deduct up to **$25,000** of passive rental losses against nonpassive income. "Active participation" is a low bar (lower than "material participation") — making management decisions like approving tenants, setting rental terms, and approving expenditures qualifies, and you can still qualify while using a property manager as long as you retain decision authority. You must own at least 10% of the activity. [IRS Pub 925; Hall CPA; Newburg CPA]

**The phaseout (income-sensitive):**

| Modified AGI (MAGI) | Allowance available |
|---|---|
| $100,000 or less | Full $25,000 |
| $100,000 – $150,000 | Reduced by $1 for every $2 over $100,000 |
| $150,000 or more | $0 (loss suspended and carried forward) |

The maximum $25,000 allowance is reduced by 50% of the amount by which MAGI exceeds $100,000, and reaches $0 at $150,000 MAGI. [IRS Pub 925] Worked IRS example: $120,000 salary with a $31,000 active-participation rental loss → deduct only $15,000 this year, carry $16,000 forward to next year. [IRS Pub 925; ourtaxpartner]

> **San Francisco / Bay Area note:** High local incomes frequently exceed the $150,000 ceiling, in which case the special allowance is fully phased out and current-year losses cannot offset wage income — they are suspended instead. A high-earning California professional at ~$180,000 cannot use this exception. [KDA Inc.]

**Filing-status trap:** If you are married filing separately and lived with your spouse at any time during the year, the special allowance is **$0**. (If you lived apart the entire year, the maximum is $12,500 with a lower phaseout range.) [IRS Pub 925; WCG CPAs; Form 8582 instructions]

### What happens to disallowed losses

Suspended passive losses carry forward until you have passive income to absorb them, the allowance becomes usable, or you **dispose of your entire interest in a fully taxable transaction** (typically a sale to an unrelated party), at which point accumulated suspended losses are generally released. They are tracked on Form 8582. [IRS Pub 925; ourtaxpartner; WCG CPAs]

### Real estate professional status (REPS)

If you qualify as a real estate professional and materially participate, the rule treating all rental activity as passive does not apply, so losses can offset wage/business income without the $25,000 cap. The bar is high (material participation plus substantial-hours requirements) and usually does not fit someone with a full-time non-real-estate job. [IRS Pub 925; Newburg CPA]

## 3. Repairs — Fully Deductible in the Current Year (federal)

Repairs that keep the property in ordinary operating condition are **fully deductible in the year incurred**, reported on Schedule E (line 14). Examples: patching drywall, fixing leaks, repainting, replacing a broken window with a similar one, replacing a broken appliance. [IRS Topic 414; Blue J; NSKT Global]

The IRS explicitly distinguishes deductible repairs from non-deductible improvements: you may not deduct the cost of improvements, which instead must be recovered through depreciation (reported via Form 4562). [IRS "Tips on rental real estate income"]

### Repairs vs. Improvements (the critical distinction)

- **Repair:** maintains current condition; does not add value, extend useful life, or adapt to a new use → deduct now. [Blue J; IRS]
- **Improvement:** must be **capitalized and depreciated** (often over 27.5 years for structural items) → deducted slowly. [IRS; Nolo]

The dollar impact is large. Classify a $10,000 roof expense as a repair → deduct $10,000 this year. Classify it as an improvement → roughly $350 this year, spread over 27.5 years. [Nolo]

The IRS uses the **"BAR test"** — **B**etterment, **A**daptation, or **R**estoration — to decide whether a cost must be capitalized, under the Tangible Property Regulations (Treas. Reg. § 1.263(a)-3). Classification can also turn on the **"unit of property"** concept, where the IRS evaluates major building systems individually: fixing one furnace component is a repair, but replacing the entire HVAC system is typically a capital improvement. [vipwealthadvisors; Blue J]

### Safe harbors that let you currently deduct otherwise-capitalizable costs

- **De minimis safe harbor.** For landlords without an applicable financial statement, items up to **$2,500 per item** (as shown on the invoice) can be deducted immediately — e.g., an $1,800 refrigerator can be expensed rather than depreciated. [Nolo; vipwealthadvisors] *(Note: some sources cite higher thresholds; $2,500 is the figure for taxpayers without an applicable financial statement — confirm the current-year amount.)*
- **Safe Harbor for Small Taxpayers (SHST).** Lets landlords currently deduct repairs/maintenance/improvements, but only for buildings with **unadjusted basis of $1 million or less**, capped at the **lesser of $10,000 or 2% of the building's unadjusted basis** per year, building-by-building. [Nolo]
- **Routine maintenance safe harbor.** Recurring upkeep to keep the building/systems in ordinary operating condition is automatically deductible in a single year. [Nolo]

> **San Francisco note:** The SHST's $1 million unadjusted-basis cap is easy to exceed on an SF property, so this safe harbor may be unavailable even though it would apply to a similar building in a lower-cost area. *(This is an inference applying the Nolo-sourced $1M cap to SF property values — not a separate citation.)*

Improvements, once capitalized, increase the property's basis — which raises future depreciation and reduces taxable gain on sale. [vipwealthadvisors; Blue J]

## 4. Mortgage Interest — Deductible (Interest Only) (federal)

Mortgage interest on a rental is deductible as a rental expense on **Schedule E, line 12 — interest only, not principal payments.** This is a straightforward business-expense deduction against rental income, distinct from (and not subject to) the personal-residence mortgage interest caps. [NSKT Global; IRS Pub 527]

**Wrinkles:**

- **Cash-out refinancing.** If you refinance for more than the previous outstanding balance and use the extra proceeds for something not related to rental use, the points/interest allocable to that portion generally cannot be deducted as a rental expense. IRS example: refinance a $100,000 balance into a $120,000 loan and use the extra $20,000 to buy a car → the points on the $20,000 are nondeductible personal interest. [IRS Pub 527]
- **Points on a same-lender refinance.** Generally not deductible in the year of refinancing, but may be deductible over the term of the new loan. [IRS Pub 527]
- **Section 163(j) business interest limitation.** A cap on business interest deductions that can apply to larger operations (recently affected by 2025 legislation, the One Big Beautiful Bill Act). A single rental almost certainly will not be affected due to a small-business gross-receipts exception, but it matters if the portfolio scales up. *(This point rests on a single secondary source; verify against IRC §163(j) / IRS guidance before relying on it. Does not affect your model — see Appendix A.)* [Uncle Kam]

## 5. Other Common Schedule E Deductions (federal)

Beyond depreciation, repairs, and interest, ordinary and necessary rental expenses are deductible, including advertising (line 5); auto and travel for property visits, repair supervision, and rent collection (line 6); cleaning and maintenance (line 7); insurance — landlord liability, property, flood/earthquake, umbrella (line 9); legal and professional fees, including CPA fees for rental tax prep and eviction attorney costs (line 10); management fees, typically 8–10% of monthly rent (line 11); property taxes (line 16); and utilities you pay (line 17). [NSKT Global; IRS Topic 414]

Net rental income is subject to income tax but **not** self-employment tax for standard long-term rentals reported on Schedule E. [NSKT Global] A rental *profit* may be subject to the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT). [IRS Topic 414]

You may also be eligible to deduct an additional 20% of qualified business income (QBI) if you meet the safe-harbor requirements of Rev. Proc. 2019-38 (Section 199A). [IRS Topic 414]

## 6. California-Specific Treatment (important for an SF owner)

**Base building depreciation largely conforms.** California generally follows the federal MACRS straight-line method and federal useful lives for property (Cal. Code Regs. tit. 18 § 24349(l); R&TC §§ 24349–24354), so the 27.5-year residential building depreciation is essentially the same on the California return. [FTB Form 3885 instructions]

**California does NOT conform to bonus depreciation, and limits Section 179.** This is the main divergence:

- California **completely disallows federal bonus depreciation** under IRC § 168(k) and requires taxpayers to **add back** any bonus depreciation claimed federally; the state has rejected every version of bonus depreciation since 2001 and did not adopt the OBBBA reinstatement of 100% bonus depreciation. [Bloomberg Tax (Cal. Rev. & Tax. Code § 24349; FTB Pub 1001); R&TC §§ 17250 / 24356; LSL CPAs; KDA Inc.]
- California **caps Section 179 expensing at $25,000**, versus the much higher federal limit. [LSL CPAs; Lifetime PM (citing FTB)]
- For an individual rental owner, this matters most if you use **bonus depreciation or a cost segregation study** on improvements (e.g., appliances, flooring, land improvements that federally qualify for bonus/accelerated treatment). You then **maintain two depreciation schedules** — one federal, one California — and report the add-back on **Schedule CA (540)**; corporations/LLCs use **Form FTB 3885**. The FTB is reportedly aggressive on depreciation-mismatch audits. [KDA Inc.; Lifetime PM; LSL CPAs]
- Practical effect: bigger deductions on the federal return in early years, smaller on the California return — a timing difference, not a permanent loss. [LSL CPAs; KDA Inc.]

**California taxes rental income (and gains) as ordinary income.** California has no preferential capital gains rate; rental income and capital gains alike are taxed at ordinary rates from **1% to 13.3%** (the top rate includes a 1% Mental Health Services Tax on income over $1 million). A California landlord earning ~$150,000 can face a combined federal-plus-California marginal rate around 37%. [Lifetime PM; Brotman Law (R&TC §17041); ustax.tools]

**San Francisco itself** does not impose a separate depreciation rule; the relevant non-federal layer is California state tax. (Note: SF does impose other property-related taxes/registration requirements outside the scope of income-tax depreciation.)

## 7. Depreciation Recapture (when you sell — plan ahead)

When you sell, the IRS **recaptures** depreciation at up to a **25% federal rate** on all depreciation **claimed or that should have been claimed.** [landlordstudio; reiprime; Greenbush]

- Under the **"allowed or allowable" rule**, your basis is reduced by depreciation you were entitled to take whether or not you actually took it. Skipping depreciation does **not** avoid recapture — it just forfeits the deduction while still owing the tax. **Therefore: always claim depreciation every year.** [reiprime; ourtaxpartner]
- If depreciation was missed in prior years, **Form 3115** (Change in Accounting Method) can be filed to claim a catch-up deduction in the current year. [reiprime]
- Depreciation lowers your cost basis, which can **increase** the taxable gain on sale (separate from, and on top of, recapture). [Greenbush]
- **California angle:** California includes depreciation recapture in the gain and taxes it as **ordinary income up to 13.3%**, with no preferential rate — so an SF owner faces 25% federal recapture **plus** California ordinary rate on the recaptured amount. [madrasaccountancy; Reed Corporation CPA]

### What applies

**Just-cause eviction + SF Rent Board fee — but NOT local rent caps.** A single-family home is exempt from SF's local rent-increase caps under the state Costa-Hawkins Act, which prohibits cities from imposing rent control on single-family dwellings and condominiums. [Costa-Hawkins / Wikipedia; LA Metro Home Finder] However, the exemption is partial: units exempted from rent-control limits by Costa-Hawkins remain subject to the Rent Ordinance for all other purposes, including the just-cause eviction regulations and payment of the annual Rent Board fee. [SF.gov — Costa-Hawkins partial exemption] So you are not limited to the ~1.6% annual increase, but you do owe the per-unit Rent Board fee (~$59/unit, half recoverable from the tenant) and cannot terminate a tenancy without cause.

**AB 1482 rent cap — avoidable, but only if you act.** The statewide cap (5% + local CPI, max 10%) is the fallback. A single-family home is exempt from AB 1482 only if it is not owned by a corporation, REIT, or LLC with a corporate member [bfpminc / AB 1482 summary] — you qualify on ownership since you hold it individually — AND you serve the required written exemption notice with the specific statutory language; without that notice the unit may lose its exemption and fall under AB 1482's cap. [structureproperties AB 1482 guide] **Action: serve the AB 1482 exemption notice**, then you can model market-rate rent growth. SF separately bars using an exorbitant rent increase to circumvent eviction protections on a SFH. [KQED]

**SF business registration (small).** Even though you are exempt from the SF gross receipts *tax* (see below), all persons doing business in San Francisco must renew their business registration each year, even if exempt from the registration fee. [SF Treasurer — Annual Business Registration]. The registration fee is small at this scale (roughly $105 at the low end depending on receipts). [countrytaxcalc SF guide]

**SF Real Property Transfer Tax (exit cost, paid by seller).** At ~$1.5M you fall in the tier where the rate is 1.15% for $1M–$5M, about $17,250 on a $1.5M sale. [countrytaxcalc SF guide] Model it as a cost of disposition.

**Passive-loss limitation — fully phased out at ~$300k income.** Above $150k MAGI the $25,000 active-participation allowance is $0, so rental losses (including depreciation) cannot offset your W-2 salary; they are suspended and carried forward until you have rental profit or sell. [IRS Pub 925] At $66k gross rent on a $1.5M property, after mortgage interest, property tax, and depreciation you will likely show a tax loss — but it will not shelter your salary in the current year.

**Net Investment Income Tax (3.8%).** At ~$300k MAGI you exceed both thresholds — $200,000 single / $250,000 married filing jointly. [IRS Topic 559] Net rental income is subject to NIIT unless you qualify as a real estate professional, and gain on selling a rental is also net investment income (the §121 primary-residence exclusion does not shield rental gain). [Arvori NIIT guide] Model 3.8% on any net rental profit and on the eventual sale gain.

**Prop 13 property tax — basis does NOT reset.** California assesses property at purchase price with increases capped at 2%/year, reassessed only on sale or change of ownership. [countrytaxcalc SF guide] Because you are converting rather than selling, there is no change of ownership and no reassessment — model your *current* assessed value growing at the ~2% cap, not a fresh $1.5M assessment.

### Conversion-specific rules (these are the new ones for you)

**Depreciable basis is capped at the lower of cost or value at conversion.** Depreciation restarts at conversion, not at what you originally paid. When you convert a personal residence to rental property, the depreciable basis is the lesser of (1) your adjusted basis on the date of conversion or (2) the property's fair market value on that date, excluding land — under IRC §168(i)(5) and Reg. §1.168(i)-4(b). [Blue J — depreciable basis on conversion; ETS] Depreciation begins when the property is placed in service as a rental (ready and available for rent), not when first rented. [Blue J — IRS guidelines on conversion] Because SF land is a large share of a $1.5M property's value, your depreciable *building* basis — and the annual deduction — will be well below $1.5M ÷ 27.5.

**Get a dated appraisal/CMA at conversion — substantiation is the taxpayer's burden.** In *Sherman Derell Smith v. Commissioner* (T.C. Memo. 2025-24), the taxpayer converted a residence to a rental and lost the depreciation deductions entirely because he could not substantiate either FMV or adjusted basis at conversion. [WCG CPAs — Smith v. Commissioner] Document the conversion-date value with a professional appraisal or comparative market analysis.

**Section 121 exclusion — a ~3-year clock that is a key decision point.** As your current primary residence, a future sale could exclude up to $250,000 of gain (single) / $500,000 (married filing jointly) under §121, but once you convert and rent, the exclusion generally applies only if the property is sold within three years of conversion (the 2-of-last-5-years use test). [LBMC — converting residence to rental; Kitces] Two limits even inside the window: depreciation claimed during the rental period is recaptured and taxed as ordinary income up to 25% and cannot be excluded under §121, and the exclusion is reduced for nonqualified-use periods. [Blue J — IRS guidelines on conversion] If this home holds significant appreciation, **the 3-year window is the most valuable item to model**: holding it as a rental beyond ~3 years forfeits the exclusion and exposes the full gain to federal capital gains + California ordinary rates (up to 13.3%) + NIIT (3.8%) + depreciation recapture (25% federal).

**Dual-basis rule on a later sale.** If you eventually sell at a loss, the IRS measures the loss using the lower of FMV on the conversion date or adjusted basis (so any value decline that occurred while it was your personal residence is never deductible); if you sell at a gain, it reverts to your original adjusted cost basis. [LegalClarity — conversion basis rules] If the sale price lands between the two basis figures, the result can be neither a deductible loss nor a taxable gain. [Porte Brown — converting a home]

### What does NOT apply (discarded, with reason)

**SF Gross Receipts Tax — exempt.** A residential lessor is a "small business enterprise" exempt from the GRT if it leases fewer than 4 units in any individual building; a single-family home is one unit. [SF Business & Tax Code §954.1] (You still register, per above.)

**SF Empty Homes / vacancy tax — not applicable and currently suspended.** Properties with only one or two residences are exempt, and separately the SF Superior Court ruled the tax unconstitutional and the City was ordered to stop enforcing it effective December 6, 2024; the City has suspended it pending appeal. [SF Examiner; SF Treasurer — Empty Homes Tax]

**Soft-story seismic retrofit — not applicable.** SF's mandatory program applies to wood-frame buildings with five or more dwelling units; buildings smaller than 5 units are not subject to mandatory retrofitting. [SF.gov — Soft Story; LL CRE]

**California LLC franchise tax — not applicable.** You hold the property individually, so there is no LLC and no annual LLC franchise tax to model.

### Your action checklist

1. Get a professional appraisal or CMA dated at the conversion (placed-in-service) date — anchors both depreciation basis and future gain/loss.
2. Serve the AB 1482 written exemption notice to the tenant (preserves market-rate rent growth on the SFH).
3. Register the rental as a business with the SF Treasurer & Tax Collector (you are GRT-exempt but registration still required).
4. Decide the §121 strategy: sell within ~3 years of conversion to use the $250k/$500k exclusion, or accept full taxation on a later sale.
5. Keep your existing Prop 13 assessed value in the model (no reassessment on conversion); add the Rent Board fee, SF transfer tax as an exit cost, and 3.8% NIIT on net rental profit and sale gain.

## Bottom-line summary

1. Depreciate the building (not land) straight-line over 27.5 years; it starts when placed in service and uses a mid-month convention. [IRS Pub 527]
2. Depreciation is always calculated, but whether a resulting loss offsets your wages depends on the passive-loss rules — the $25,000 active-participation allowance phases out from $100K–$150K MAGI and disappears above $150K, a real constraint at SF income levels. [IRS Pub 925; KDA Inc.]
3. Repairs are deducted in full the same year; improvements must be capitalized and depreciated — the classification (BAR test, safe harbors) materially affects your current deduction. [IRS; Nolo; Blue J]
4. Mortgage interest is deductible (interest only, not principal) on Schedule E line 12. [NSKT Global; IRS Pub 527]
5. California conforms to the 27.5-year building depreciation but disallows bonus depreciation and caps §179 at $25,000, so you may track two schedules and add back on Schedule CA (540) / FTB 3885; California also taxes rental income and recapture as ordinary income up to 13.3%. [FTB 3885 instructions; Bloomberg Tax; LSL CPAs; KDA Inc.]
6. Always claim depreciation — recapture at sale (25% federal + CA ordinary rate) applies whether or not you claimed it. [reiprime; ourtaxpartner; Reed Corporation CPA]
7. For an individually owned SF single-family home being converted from a primary residence (Section 9): rent caps don't apply (Costa-Hawkins) but just-cause + Rent Board fee do; AB 1482's cap is avoidable by serving the exemption notice; GRT, Empty Homes Tax, soft-story retrofit, and LLC franchise tax do not apply; depreciable basis is the lower of cost or value at conversion (get an appraisal); the §121 gain exclusion runs out ~3 years after conversion; and at ~$300k income the passive-loss allowance is fully phased out while the 3.8% NIIT applies. [SF.gov; structureproperties; SF Business & Tax Code §954.1; Blue J; LBMC; IRS Topic 559]

*Not tax advice. The repair-vs-improvement classification and the federal/California timing differences in particular warrant review by a CPA, especially given Bay Area property values and income levels.*
