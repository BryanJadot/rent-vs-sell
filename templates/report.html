2. What each path asks of you

First the non-financial trade-offs (effort, liquidity, obligations), then the real cash each path takes from or puts in your pocket year by year. Stated as facts for each side, not advice.

Holding means…
  • running a rental — tenants, repairs, turnover, management
  • funding a monthly cash drain out of savings (below) until the loan is paid off
  • wealth concentrated in one mortgaged, illiquid property
  • landlord duties under California's AB 1482 rent-cap law (keep the exemption by serving the required written notice) and just-cause eviction; a tenant-occupied home is harder to sell vacant
  • the gain taxed later, at the future sale
Selling means…
  • managing a liquid, diversifiable portfolio instead of a property
  • no monthly property cash to fund and no tenants to manage
  • proceeds spreadable across many holdings; value swings with the market
  • no landlord or just-cause obligations once sold
  • the gain (if any) taxed now (detail in §3)

3. Show your work — how a number is built

A few money words, in plain English

  • Market return — what cash would earn if invested in the stock market instead ({{ '%g'|format(primary_invest*100) }}%/yr here). Money tied up elsewhere "costs" you this forgone return.
  • §121 exclusion — a tax break that shields the first ${{ '{:g}'.format(cg_exclusion/1000) }}k of gain on a home you've lived in (2 of the last 5 yrs); a pure rental doesn't get it.
  • Depreciation — a yearly paper deduction landlords take for wear-and-tear. At high income it can't be used yearly (it's suspended) and is applied later when you sell.
  • Depreciation recapture — at sale, the IRS taxes back those depreciation deductions ({{ '%.1f'|format(deprec_recapture_rate*100) }}% here).
  • Future dollars — figures aren't inflation-adjusted: at {{ '%g'|format(inflation_pct) }}% inflation, $1 in {{ longest_horizon }} yrs is worth ~{{ '%.0f'|format(today_value_pct) }}¢ today. Both paths use the same future dollars, so they stay comparable.
  • Pre-tax / after-tax — "pre-tax" is before tax is taken out, "after-tax" is what's left once it's paid. Both paths grow pre-tax and pay tax once at the end, so the comparison is fair.

How the rental-tax pieces interact. While renting, the property runs a tax loss each year (rent minus operating costs, mortgage interest, and ~${{ '{:,.0f}'.format(annual_depreciation) }}/yr depreciation). At income >${{ '{:g}'.format(passive_loss_magi_limit/1000) }}k those losses can't be deducted yearly — they're suspended and released at sale, deducted at the {{ '%.0f'|format(marginal_tax*100) }}% rate (+${{ '{:,.0f}'.format(we.deprec_release) }}). At sale the IRS also recaptures the depreciation at {{ '%.1f'|format(deprec_recapture_rate*100) }}% (fed 25% + NIIT 3.8% + CA 13.3%) (−${{ '{:,.0f}'.format(we.recapture) }}). The released losses (+${{ '{:,.0f}'.format(we.deprec_release) }}) minus the recapture (−${{ '{:,.0f}'.format(we.recapture) }}) net to {{ deprec_net }}. They don't fully cancel: recapture accrues on every year of depreciation, while the released loss pool is drawn down once the rental turns tax-positive in later years. None of this affects monthly cash flow; it lands at sale. Confirm with a CPA.
