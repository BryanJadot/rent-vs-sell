A few money words, in plain English
  • Market return — what cash would earn if invested in the stock market instead ({{ '%g'|format(primary_invest*100) }}%/yr here). Money tied up elsewhere "costs" you this forgone return.
  • §121 exclusion — a tax break that shields the first ${{ '{:g}'.format(cg_exclusion/1000) }}k of gain on a home you've lived in (2 of the last 5 yrs); a pure rental doesn't get it.
  • Depreciation — a yearly paper deduction landlords take for wear-and-tear. At high income it can't be used yearly (it's suspended) and is applied later when you sell.
  • Depreciation recapture — at sale, the IRS taxes back those depreciation deductions ({{ '%.1f'|format(deprec_recapture_rate*100) }}% here).
  • Pre-tax / after-tax — both paths grow money pre-tax and pay the tax once at the end, so the comparison is fair.

2. What each path asks of you

Beyond the dollars — the non-financial trade-offs. Stated as facts for each side, not advice.

Holding means…
  • running a rental — tenants, repairs, turnover, management
  • funding a monthly cash drain out of savings (below) until the loan is paid off
  • wealth concentrated in one mortgaged, illiquid property
  • landlord duties: AB 1482 rent-cap (keep the exemption by serving the written notice) and just-cause eviction; a tenant-occupied home is harder to sell vacant
  • the gain taxed later, at the future sale
Selling means…
  • managing a liquid, diversifiable portfolio instead of a property
  • no monthly property cash to fund and no tenants to manage
  • proceeds spreadable across many holdings; value swings with the market
  • no landlord or just-cause obligations once sold
  • the gain (if any) taxed now (detail in §4)
