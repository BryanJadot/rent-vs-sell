How to read this

This compares two paths for one house:

  • Sell now: sell today, pay the costs and taxes, invest what's left in the market.
  • Hold: keep it and rent it out, then sell later — paying the selling costs and taxes at that future sale too.

Each number is the total wealth a path leaves you with at a given future year ({{ shortest_horizon }}–{{ longest_horizon }} yrs out), after all costs and taxes on both sides, so the two are comparable. Figures are nominal future dollars (not inflation-adjusted): at {{ '%g'|format(inflation_pct) }}% inflation, a dollar {{ longest_horizon }} years out is worth about {{ '%.0f'|format(today_value_pct) }}¢ in today's money — but since both paths are in the same future dollars, they stay on a common basis. This page is computed data only: figures, sensitivities, and how each number is built. It draws no conclusion about which choice to make.

4. Facts to know if renting it out

Legal/process facts that the dollar model does not capture. Stated as facts, not advice.

  • AB 1482 rent cap exemption. A single-family home is exempt from California's statewide rent cap (Costa-Hawkins) only if the owner serves the required written exemption notice; without it the unit can fall under the cap (5% + CPI). The rent-growth figures here assume market-rate growth.
  • Just-cause eviction. An SF single-family rental is subject to just-cause eviction rules. A home occupied by a tenant is generally harder to sell with vacant possession; selling vacant typically requires timing a sale around a tenant move-out.
  • Concentration. Holding keeps ~${{ '%.2f'|format(home_value/1e6) }}M of net worth in a single, leveraged asset; selling and investing distributes it across a portfolio. The model prices neither the timing constraint nor concentration.
  • Cited reference. Tax and eviction details: docs/sf-rental-tax-reference.md.
